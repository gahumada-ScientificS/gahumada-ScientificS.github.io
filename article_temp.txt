Abstract
Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) is a commodity chemical used broadly in the industrial sector that
heavily relies on the 80+ year old anthraquinone process, requiring signiﬁcant energy input in
combination with precious metal catalyzed hydrogenation. We describe a metal-free molecular
cyclopentadiene-iminium ([CpN3H]2+) electrocatalyst for producing H2O2 under ambient conditions
with high Faradaic eﬃciency (up to 95%) and turnover frequency (TOF up to 104 s-1), surpassing
some of the most active transition-metal based molecular catalyst systems. The amine-rich radical
cation intermediate ([CpN3H]+•) transfers a potent hydrogen atom (H•) to O2 through an unusual
radical-rebound mechanism to form a key hydroperoxyl intermediate ([CpN3OOH]+•), with cutting-
edge computational (DFT) and experimental mechanistic analyses in excellent agreement. This work
demonstrates an innovative and sustainable molecular approach to furnish a valuable commodity
chemical through an electrically driven process without the need for transition metals.

Introduction 
The development of redox processes that sustainably deliver protons and electrons to small 
molecules is critical for the energy and commodity chemical sectors. There is an urgent need to 
electrify existing thermal and/or fossil-fuel based chemical processes through the direct 
electroreduction of small molecules such as N2, O2, and/or CO2 in the presence of efficient and 
sustainable (electro)catalysts, linking renewable energy reservoirs (solar, wind, etc.) with chemical 
production pipelines.1-5 While heterogeneous catalyst research and development is actively being 
pursued to address these challenges,6 there has been widespread interest in studying soluble 
electrocatalysts in chemical synthesis because molecular scaffolds enable fidelity at the atomic 
level to maximize product selectivity and efficiency through iterative chemical design strategies.7-
9 
Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) is produced annually from O2 on a megaton scale and is a widely 
used reagent for paper and textile bleaching,10, 11 water sewage treatment,12 and chemical 
synthesis,13 thus securing its place as one of the 100 most important chemicals for industry and 
society.14 In this context, the anthraquinone process remains the industry standard15 for performing 
the necessary oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) to H2O2 via the heterogeneous Pd-catalyzed 
hydrogenation of an anthraquinone derivative (AQR) followed by solution-phase autoxidation of 
 
3 
the anthrahydroquinone (AH2QR) under O2 to generate H2O2 in high yield (Figure 1A). However, 
this process poses several technical challenges arising from inefficiencies in the 
hydrogenation/oxidation reactors, quinone overhydrogenation, organic product contamination 
during product extraction, and the need for extensive investment in industrial purification 
equipment.13 Some electrochemical H2O2 production processes based on the oxidative coupling of 
SO42- are also known,15 but their relevance is dwarfed by the anthraquinone process; however, 
recent developments in solid electrolyte fuel-cells and redox-mediated anthraquinone technologies 
have revitalized the prospects of attaining scalable electrochemical ORR production processes.16-
18 Nonetheless, research into alternative (electro)synthetic ORR production methods is needed to 
sidestep the limitations of the anthraquinone process and increase the overall sustainability of this 
critical feedstock chemical.

To further understand electrically-driven molecular ORR and gain deeper mechanistic insights 
into the 2H+/2e- reduction to H2O2, a diverse array of molecular electrocatalyst architectures have 
been investigated in detail.19-21 Electrocatalysts containing transition metals (Mn), which have been 
most heavily studied, typically undergo inner-sphere O2 binding to generate Mn+1-OO• 
“superoxide” intermediates in the initial stages of reduction (Figure 1B).22, 23 Conversely, metal-
free alternatives are substantially rarer and operate via mechanistically divergent pathways. For 
example, Gabbaï and co-workers demonstrated that chemically tethered carbenium dications 
cooperatively activate O2, selectively catalyzing H2O2 formation under chemical and 
electrochemical conditions using methanesulfonic acid as the proton source (Figure 1C).24 
Pioneering work by Savéant and Hapiot showed that methylviologen ([MV]+) and acridinium salts 
generate O2•- via outer sphere electron transfer (ET) to O2 under acidic reducing conditions, 
followed by HO2• generation and rapid disproportionation to yield O2 and H2O2 (Figure 1D).25-27 
More recently, Machan and co-workers reported an iminium based electrocatalyst (im) whose 
mechanism of ORR depends on the method of electroreduction: under spectrochemical conditions, 
outer-sphere ET generates O2•- (Figure 1E) while an electrochemical bias promotes inner-sphere 
O2 binding to a carbon-based organoradical followed by a 4H+/4e- reduction to generate H2O.28 
Inner-sphere activation of O2 has also been demonstrated with a phenanthrolinium radical cation

a hydroperoxyl cation intermediate ([ROOH]+), which subsequently releases H2O2.29 Notably,

In our previous work, we discovered that [CpN3H]2+, a redox-active dication with delocalized 
iminium-alkene character, has a moderately acidic C-H bond (pKaMeCN = 11.7; Fig 1F, left).35 
Subsequent 1e- reduction to [CpN3H]•+ yields a weak homolytic C-H bond dissociation free energy 
(BDFECH = 52 kcal/mol), prompting us to explore the utility of [CpN3H]•+ as a proton-coupled 
electron transfer (PCET) reagent for the activation of O2 since: (1) the driving force for generating 
the aqueous hydroperoxyl radical (HO2•) from O2 is comparable (BDFEOH = 51.2 kcal/mol);19 (2) 
the presumed electrophilic character of radical cation [CpN3H]•+ would be suitably matched to react 
with a nucleophilic radical substrate such as O2.36 Herein, we report a metal-free electrocatalyst 
that rapidly and selectively facilitates the homogenous 2H+/2e- reduction of O2 to H2O2 with 
[CpN3H]2+ serving as a precatalyst in the presence of an exogenous proton donor (Fig 1F, right). 
Experimental and computational (DFT) mechanistic studies are in excellent agreement, supporting 
a pathway where H-atom transfer from [CpN3H]•+ to an incoming O2 molecule is followed by a 
radical-rebound to generate a hydroperoxide radical intermediate [CpN3(OOH)]+ and subsequent 
CPET steps to regenerate [CpN3H]•+. While the stoichiometric reaction of O2 and precious metals 
to generate hydroperoxide (M-OOH) complexes via H-atom transfer (HAT) is well documented,37-
42 analogous HAT pathways using metal-free systems remain unprecedented, to the best of our 
knowledge. Overall, this work demonstrates a novel “metal-like” electrocatalytic ORR pathway 
traversed by the [CpN3H]•+ organoradical to facilitate H2O2 production via an initial HAT, deviating 
 
6 
from commonly encountered mechanistic scenarios involving outer-sphere and/or inner-sphere O2 
activation pathways. 
Results and Discussion 
To begin, cyclic voltammograms (CVs) of the redox-active [CpN3H]2+ under N2 (E1/2 = -0.72 V 
vs. Fc+/0, MeCN) become irreversible when the solution is saturated with air (Figure S5; all 
potentials in this study are reported vs. Fc+/0). Addition of excess [p-BrC6H4NH3][BF4] (pKaMeCN = 
9.43)43 leads to a substantial increase in current, suggestive of electrocatalytic O2 reduction 
behavior (Figure 2A, orange trace).44 Switching from air to a dry stream of O2 leads to an 
approximate five-fold increase in peak current, consistent with a concomitant increase in O2 
solubility45 and a first-order dependence on [O2] (green trace). Negligible current enhancement is 
observed with a Et3NH+ (pKaMeCN = 18.82),43 a weaker acid than [CpN3H]2+ (Figure S5).35 
Furthermore, the redox chemistry of [CpN3]+ in the absence of O2 and acid is too cathodic (< -1.4 
V) with respect to the electrochemical windows explored in this study, ruling out [CpN3]• as an on-
cycle intermediate (Figure S6). The addition of exogenous H2O to the system has little effect on 
the current response, indicating that proton relays or hydrogen bonding effects do not meaningfully 
contribute to the observed reactivity of [CpN3H]2+ with H+ and O2 in solution (Fig S20).46 Due to 
the known electrochemical irreversibility of [CpN3]+ under oxidizing conditions when using 
[nBu4N][PF6] as the supporting electrolyte,47 the more inert supporting electrolyte 
[nBu4N][B(C6F5)4] was used for all CV experiments to mitigate possible catalyst degradation 
stemming from reactions with PF6-.

Relevant control electrolysis experiments strongly indicate that [CpN3H]2+ is indeed selectively 
catalyzing the production of H2O2, as in the absence of O2 or acid, no H2O2 is detected and 
negligible current is passed (Figure 2, right). Only trace amounts of H2 were detected in the 
headspace via gas chromatography when excluding O2 (Table S1). In the absence of [CpN3H]2+, 
H2O2 is generated with 14% FE. H2O2 production in the absence of catalyst presumably occurs via 
background O2 reduction in acidic aprotic solvent,25 as evidenced by modest increases in current 
in bulk electrolysis experiments (Figure 2B). Notably, the addition of the hydroperoxyl (HO2•) 
radical trap 5,5-dimethyl-1pyrroline N-oxide (DMPO) in CV experiments has no effect on the 
current response (Figure S19, left),51 indicating that the formation of O2•- followed by free 
hydroperoxyl radical (HO2•) formation and rapid disproportionation to generate H2O2 and H2O25 is 
likely not occurring. Moreover, computational modeling of the reaction between DMPO and HO2•  
at the PWPB95-D4/def2-QZVPP/CPCM-DRACO(MeCN)//r2SCAN-3c/CPCM(MeCN) level of 
theory (see Supporting Information, part B for computational details) yields a low transition state 
barrier height of 8.8 kcal/mol (Figure S32), which further supports the absence of free HO2• in 
solution as it should readily be intercepted by the large excess of DMPO in the reaction medium.

To obtain kinetics data for this metal-free ORR reaction to produce H2O2, a series of CV 
experiments were conducted with varying acid concentrations, showing a clear dependence on 
current response with increasing [H+] (Figure 3A). All CV waveforms are well within zone K,8, 52-
54 which is commonly seen in rapid oxygen reduction electrocatalysis due to substrate (O2) 
depletion in the diffusion layer. We have previously shown compelling evidence for HAT via 
[CpN3H]•+ under reducing conditions, as the radical cation can transfer H• to TEMPO or succumb 
to deactivation via intermolecular H-atom transfer (i.e., radical-radical disproportionation) 
involving two equivalents of [CpN3H]•+ (Figures 4-5).35 The bimolecular deactivation scenario is 
corroborated by two key observations when conducting CVs of [CpN3H]2+ under N2 with excess 
H+: (1) increased redox reversibility with increasing scan rate, suggesting that shorter CV 
timescales begin to outcompete the second-order disproportionation reaction (Figure S17); (2) 
decreased redox reversibility with increased concentration of [CpN3H]2+, which is a characteristic 
feature of electrochemically initiated radical-radical dimerization events (Figure S18).55 Moreover, 
decreased catalytic activity is observed with higher concentrations of [CpN3H]2+ with no increase 
in catalytic current above 2.0 mM, suggesting the occurrence of kinetically competent second-
order catalyst deactivation (Figure S16).29 Thus, all CV kinetic investigations are performed with 
lower concentrations of [CpN3H]2+ (0.2-0.9 mM) to both minimize catalyst deactivation and 
increase the O2:catalyst ratio (i.e., pseudo-first-order conditions with respect to [CpN3H]2+).

Since this ORR electrocatalyst deviates from an idealized S-shaped current response, CV traces 
were subjected to foot-of-the-wave analysis (FOWA)56 with varying concentrations of H+, O2, and 
[CpN3H]2+, with [H+] dependence data from -0.55 to -0.61 V results shown in Figure 3B. Based on 
this result and our proposed DFT-computed mechanism described later, FOWA parameters are 
used assuming that all electron transfer events occur at the electrode (eq S5). The Ecat/2 for 
[CpN3H]2+ at different acid concentrations is consistently -0.71 V, with no observed redox potential 
drift as a function of [H+] (Figure S7).57 Plotting kobs vs. [H+] at low acid concentrations results in 
a linear response and the plot of log[kobs] vs. log[H+] exhibits a slope of 1, indicating a first-order 
dependence on acid with an average kacid = (3.3 ± 3.6) × 107 M-1s-1 (Figure S10). Acid-saturation 
kinetics are achieved when [H+] ≥ 9 mM and averaging these data points gives kobs = TOF = 
(6.1±2.3) × 104 s-1. Consequently, [CpN3H]2+ is among the fastest molecular H2O2 production 
electrocatalysts reported in the literature, operating with an effective overpotential (ηeff) of 0.80 V 
(eqs S11-S12). For comparison, the best-performing Co(macrocycle) ORR systems achieve TOFs 
of up to 4.5 × 103 s-1 (ηeff = 0.56 V)58 while the recently reported metal-free phenanthrolindiium 
catalyst reaches TOFs of up to 1.73 × 104 s-1 (ηeff = 0.78 V).29,59 Importantly, homoconjugation 
effects60 were negligible since electrocatalytic trials in 20 mM buffered solutions containing 1:1 
[p-BrC6H4NH3]+: p-BrC6H4NH2 showed no substantial change in current response and kobs (Figure 
S11). Subjecting [CpN3H]2+ to classical icat/ip kinetic analysis results in an estimated kobs  of 9.2 ×

With the above kinetic information in hand, a computational analysis was undertaken to better 
understand the mechanism of metal-free ORR, for which we employed our previously validated 
state-of-the-art DFT workflow, capable of reproducing the known experimental PCET 
thermochemistry of CpN3 with high fidelity.47 Hence, we report all DFT-computed energies at the 
PWPB95-D4/def2-QZVPP/CPCM-DRACO(MeCN) // r2SCAN-3c/CPCM(MeCN) level of theory 
based on molecular structures obtained after thorough conformational sampling with both GOAT64 
and CREST,65 leveraging the software packages xTB,66 CENSO,67 and ORCA68 (see Supporting 
Information part B for computational details).  
Figure 4 shows our proposed catalytic cycle for ORR with precatalyst [CpN3H]2+ at an applied 
potential of -0.77 V vs. Fc+/0, wholly consistent with the experimental observations described 
above.  [CpN3H]2+ is initially reduced to the catalytically active [CpN3H]•+ (E°DFT = -0.74 V vs. Fc+/0, 
MeCN), which can perform a direct HAT to O2 with a surprisingly low barrier of 12.5 kcal/mol 
for a non-metal species, comparable to the HAT barrier from the metal hydride of CpFeH(CO)2 to 
the tris(p-tert-butylphenyl)methyl radical at room temperature (ΔG‡ = 11.9 kcal/mol).69 HAT from 
[CpN3H]•+ to O2 outcompetes the known second-order radical recombination reactivity of 
[CpN3H]•+,35 which we propose as a possible catalyst deactivation pathway with a barrier height of 
20.1 kcal/mol (Figure 4, middle) that corresponds to an effective half-life time of only t1/2 = 1.0 
min at room temperature (assuming a unit transmission coefficient in Eyring transition state 
theory).70 After successful HAT to O2, the resulting loosely bound hydroperoxyl radical cage pair 
[CpN3 • HO2]•+ can readily rearrange to the hydroperoxide [CpN3OOH]•+, completing the radical-
 
14 
rebound steps. Notably, any other hydroperoxide adducts besides the proposed [CpN3OOH]•+ (e.g., 
with the hydroperoxyl group bound to an adjacent carbon atom) were ruled out based on both 
kinetic and thermodynamic arguments (see Section III in the SI, part B, for a detailed discussion). 
Furthermore, the formation of radical cage pairs via HAT and subsequent radical recombination is 
akin to stoichiometric O2 activation reactions mediated by a Au(III) hydride complex to form a 
diamagnetic Au-OOH adduct.42, 71 Upon formation of the hydroperoxyl intermediate [CpN3OOH]•+, 
we propose that two sequential CPET steps regenerate the catalytically active [CpN3H] •+ at standard 
reduction potentials of +0.42 V and –0.45 V vs. Fc+/0, respectively.

Instead of two subsequent CPET steps to close the catalytic cycle, one could also envision two 
PCET pathways via EC (electrochemical followed by chemical step) or CE pathways (vice versa; 
Figure 5). However, both these options are less likely for kinetic and thermodynamic reasons. 
First, the maximum applied potential of -0.77 V vs. Fc+/0 is too high to facilitate the reduction of 
either [CpN3OOH]•+ (E°DFT = -0.95 V vs. Fc+/0) or [CpN3]+ (E°DFT = -1.43 V vs. Fc+/0), rendering the 
EC pathways electrochemically unfeasible, with the latter of these computed redox potentials also 
in excellent agreement with experiment (Figure S6). Second, protonation of [CpN3OOH]•+ is 
associated with a large barrier height of 27.1 kcal/mol, which makes this chemical step of the first 
CE pathway kinetically improbable. In fact, experiments and computations corroborate that there 
is no significant change in rate when employing a stronger ([p-CNC6H4NH3][BF4], pKaMeCN = 7.0) 
or weaker acid ([C6H5NH3][BF4], pKaMeCN = 10.62), further supporting that proton transfer is not 
involved in turnover-determining step(s) during catalysis (Figures S8-S9, Table S3). Third, a 
kinetic isotope effect (KIE) of 4.6 was determined experimentally using [p-BrC6H4ND3]+ for the 
investigated ORR with [CpN3H]2+, indicating that the transfer of hydrogen (as H• in TS2 or H+ in 
TS5) is likely rate-limiting during electrocatalysis (Figure 6). However, the calculated value for 
the protonation of [CpN3]+ (KIEDFT(TS5) ~ 1.4) differs quite substantially from the experimental 
value, implying that this transition state and thus the second CE pathway should not be turnover 
limiting. However, we note that the KIE mismatch does not, by itself, definitively diagnose a non-
 
17 
turnover-limiting pathway: the mismatch may also arise due to contributions from quantum 
tunnelling (which are beyond the scope of this study), which is known to influence H/D isotope 
effects even when the KIE values appear to be within “classical” ranges.72, 73 Last and most 
importantly, it is well-known that CPETs may constitute a kinetically advantageous alternative 
pathway compared to high-energy/high-barrier stepwise elementary reactions, further supporting 
the plausibility of our current mechanistic proposal via two fast sequential CPET steps for catalyst 
regeneration.22, 74, 75 With these CPET steps in mind, catalytic turnover according to the energetic 
span model76, 77 is governed by the direct HAT to O2 (TS2) for which the calculated KIE is also in 
excellent agreement with experiment (see Table 1). 
In conclusion, we have discovered a new metal-free CpN3 electrocatalyst that rapidly and 
selectively reduces O2 to H2O2 in the presence of exogenous acid, representing an innovative HAT 
pathway towards sustainably producing a critical commodity chemical. Within our experimental 
and (semiclassical) DFT framework, the combined thermodynamic, kinetic, and isotope-effect 
evidence are consistent with the mechanistic model proposed in Figure 4. These findings 
underscore several unique features of the CpN3 family of molecules: redox reversibility, propensity 
for HAT chemistry, inner-sphere binding of a hydroperoxo moiety, and subsequent CPET 
reactivity. These exciting developments open new vistas into the metal-free electrochemical 
generation of H2O2, and we envision that the synthetically tunable CpN3 core will rapidly lead to 
significant advancements in metal-free H2O2 electrosynthesis methodologies.

Synthesis of [CpN3H][BF4]235  
In an N2-filled glove box, [CpN3][BF4]78, 79 (200 mg, 0.45 mmol) was dissolved in DCM (ca. 4 mL) 
and 1.1 equiv of HBF4•Et2O (70 µl, 0.51 mmol) was added while stirring. The blue solution turned 
yellow immediately after addition of acid and was stirred for 2 h. Diethyl ether (ca. 8 mL) was 
added to the reaction and the product precipitated as a yellow solid. The solid was washed with 
diethyl ether (ca. 2 mL), dried under high vacuum, then redissolved in MeCN (ca. 0.5 mL). The 
product was crystallized by vapor diffusion of diethyl ether into a concentrated acetonitrile solution 
in the glovebox freezer (-25 °C) overnight. Decanting the mother liquor and drying under high 
vacuum yielded the crystalline, air stable product in 92% yield (220 mg). The 1H NMR spectrum 
(Figure S3) is consistent with previously published data.35 
 
Cyclic Voltammetry 
Cyclic voltammetry (CV) experiments were conducted under N2 at 295 ± 3 K or under O2 at 295 
± 3 K in a 5-neck pear-shaped flask (Figure S26, left) using a standard three-electrode setup 
consisting of a PEEK-encased glassy carbon working disk electrode (∅ = 2 mm), Type 2 glassy 
carbon rod counter electrode (∅ = 6 mm), and Ag/AgCl pseudoreference electrode stored in a glass 
compartment containing solvent and electrolyte (0.1 M). The pseudoreference electrode is 
separated from the bulk solution using a porous glass frit (Coralpor®). All CV experiments were 
conducted in MeCN. CVs under N2 were conducted in a nitrogen-filled glove box, where the 
working electrode was polished with 0.25 μm diamond polishing paste, lapping oil, and a rayon 
micro cloth pad (Buehler) and thoroughly rinsed with MeCN.  For CVs under O2, a 0.05 µM 
alumina powder suspension in deionized (DI) water was deposited onto a rayon microfiber cloth 
 
19 
pad to polish the working electrode under air and was thoroughly rinsed with DI water and MeCN 
before use. A Gamry Reference 1010B potentiostat and Gamry software were used for data 
collection and analysis. In all reported CVs, the 4th scan/sweep is typically plotted and used for 
analysis. All CVs are referenced to the Fc+/0 redox couple (0 V). During CV experiments, ferrocene 
was added at the end of the experiments unless explicitly stated otherwise. For further details see
